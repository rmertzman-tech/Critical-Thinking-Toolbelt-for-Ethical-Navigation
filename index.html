How to Use This Resource

The Critical Thinking Toolbelt is a course-long reference for the reasoning skills used in EBB. It explains traditional critical thinking terms such as claim, assumption, inference, evidence, deduction, induction, abduction, validity, soundness, fallacy, counterexample, objection, and reply. These terms help you examine how you are thinking before you decide what to believe or do.

Use this resource when you complete Field Scans, create Move Cards, prepare MCCTS arguments, write reflections, participate in discussion, or revise your public reasoning. The goal is not to memorize vocabulary for its own sake. The goal is to use critical thinking tools to notice assumptions, test reasons, compare possible moves, repair weak reasoning, and make more responsible ethical judgments.

Relationship to FDN-1: FDN-1 introduces the practice on Day One through an interactive lab. EBB-CT-2 supports the practice all semester as a reference, quiz review, workbook support, and MCCTS/Navigator bridge.

Core Critical Thinking Terms

Common Fallacies as Ethical Field Distortions

A fallacy is a common pattern of weak or misleading reasoning. In EBB, fallacies are not merely textbook mistakes. They are often field distortions: they narrow perception, misdescribe agency, suppress relevant voices, or make poor Move Cards seem reasonable.

The Critical Thinking Check for Field Scans

Each Field Scan can include a short Critical Thinking Check.

  1. My current claim or concern is: What am I thinking, concluding, or worrying about?
  2. My reasons are: Why do I think this?
  3. My evidence is: What supports this?
  4. My assumptions are: What am I taking for granted?
  5. My inference is: How am I moving from what I notice to what I conclude?
  6. An alternative explanation is: What else might be going on?
  7. A possible fallacy or distortion is: Is there any weak reasoning, bias, or field distortion in my first reaction?
  8. A more careful version of my claim is: How should I revise the claim after thinking more carefully?

Critical Thinking and Move Cards

A Move Card is a possible next action. Each Move Card should be tested with critical thinking.

Possible move
What could I do?
Reason for the move
Why might this be a good move?
Type of reasoning
Deduction / induction / abduction / analogy / practical judgment.
Fields affected
Which life-fields would this move affect?
Possible risk
What could go wrong?
Objection
What would a thoughtful critic say?
Reply or revision
How could I improve the move?
Decision
Keep / revise / delay / reject.

Critical Thinking and SAGE

See

Skills: noticing salience; identifying assumptions; defining the issue; distinguishing observation from interpretation.

What is happening? What am I noticing first? What might I be missing?

Analyze

Skills: identifying reasons; evaluating evidence; testing inferences; considering stakeholders; identifying fallacies.

What reasons support this interpretation? What evidence matters? What assumptions shape the case?

Generate

Skills: creating alternatives; analogical reasoning; abduction; counterexample testing; Move Card design.

What options are possible? What else might explain this? What would someone with another PRF suggest?

Engage

Skills: practical judgment; argument revision; objection and reply; public reasoning; accountability.

What move should I try? How should I explain it? What feedback would make me revise?

Critical Thinking and MCCTS

MCCTS helps students move from private reflection to public argument using Toulmin-style structure.

ElementQuestionExample
ClaimWhat are you arguing?The hospital should create a protected voice channel for nurses.
GroundsWhat facts, observations, or case details support the claim?Nurses may notice safety problems before administrators do.
WarrantWhy do those grounds support the claim?People closest to a risk often have morally relevant knowledge.
BackingWhat ethical theory, concept, or course idea supports the warrant?Epistemic justice, care ethics, and institutional responsibility.
QualifierHow strong or limited is the claim?This does not mean every warning is automatically correct.
RebuttalWhat objection or complication should be considered?Some warnings may be mistaken or incomplete.
ReplyHow would you answer the objection or revise your claim?The channel should include review standards, not automatic acceptance.
